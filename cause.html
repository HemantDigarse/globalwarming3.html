What causes Global Warming?

Global warming occurs when carbon dioxide (CO2) and other air pollutants collect in the atmosphere and absorb sunlight and solar radiation that have bounced off the earth’s surface. Normally this radiation would escape into space, but these pollutants, which can last for years to centuries in the atmosphere, trap the heat and cause the planet to get hotter. These heat-trapping pollutants—specifically carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, water vapor, and synthetic fluorinated gases—are known as greenhouse gases, and their impact is called the greenhouse effect.

Though natural cycles and fluctuations have caused the earth’s climate to change several times over the last 800,000 years, our current era of global warming is directly attributable to human activity—specifically to our burning of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, gasoline, and natural gas, which results in the greenhouse effect. In the United States, the largest source of greenhouse gases is transportation (29 percent), followed closely by electricity production (28 percent) and industrial activity (22 percent). Learn about the natural and human causes of climate change.

Curbing dangerous climate change requires very deep cuts in emissions, as well as the use of alternatives to fossil fuels worldwide. The good news is that countries around the globe have formally committed—as part of the 2015 Paris Climate Agreement—to lower their emissions by setting new standards and crafting new policies to meet or even exceed those standards. The not-so-good news is that we’re not working fast enough. To avoid the worst impacts of climate change, scientists tell us that we need to reduce global carbon emissions by as much as 40 percent by 2030. For that to happen, the global community must take immediate, concrete steps: to decarbonize electricity generation by equitably transitioning from fossil fuel–based production to renewable energy sources like wind and solar; to electrify our cars and trucks; and to maximize energy efficiency in our buildings, appliances, and industries.

These are the Some Major Activity of Human that causes Global Warming:

Deforestation

Deforestation contributes to global warming primarily through the release of carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere and the reduction in the Earth's capacity to absorb CO2. Here's how deforestation causes global warming:

Carbon Dioxide Emissions: Trees act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO2 from the atmosphere during photosynthesis and storing carbon in their trunks, branches, leaves, and roots. When forests are cleared or burned, the stored carbon is released back into the atmosphere as CO2. This process significantly increases the concentration of CO2, a major greenhouse gas, contributing to the greenhouse effect and global warming.

Reduced Carbon Sequestration: Deforestation diminishes the Earth's capacity to absorb CO2 from the atmosphere. Forests play a crucial role in capturing and storing carbon, acting as natural carbon sinks. By reducing the forest cover, we reduce the planet's ability to sequester CO2, leading to higher atmospheric concentrations of this greenhouse gas.

Albedo Effect: Trees and forests also influence the Earth's albedo, which is the amount of sunlight that is reflected back into space. Forests generally have a lower albedo than other types of land cover, meaning they absorb more sunlight and heat. When forests are replaced by lighter-colored surfaces, such as bare ground or crops, more sunlight is reflected back into space, contributing to local cooling but potentially increasing warming globally due to reduced absorption of solar radiation.

Feedback Loops: Deforestation can trigger positive feedback loops that exacerbate global warming. For example, the loss of forests can lead to changes in local climate patterns, such as reduced rainfall, which further stresses the remaining forests and increases the risk of wildfires. These wildfires release additional CO2 into the atmosphere, further contributing to global warming.

Loss of Biodiversity: While not directly related to global warming, deforestation also leads to the loss of biodiversity, which can have cascading effects on ecosystems and their ability to adapt to and mitigate the impacts of climate change.

Overall, deforestation is a significant driver of global warming, accounting for a substantial portion of anthropogenic CO2 emissions. Protecting and restoring forests is crucial for mitigating climate change and preserving biodiversity.

Industrial processes

Industrial processes contribute to global warming primarily through the emission of greenhouse gases, particularly carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide (N2O). These emissions result from various industrial activities that involve the production and consumption of energy, manufacturing, and chemical processes. Here's how industrial processes cause global warming:

Burning Fossil Fuels: Many industrial activities rely on the combustion of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas to generate heat and power. This combustion releases large amounts of CO2 into the atmosphere, a major contributor to the greenhouse effect and global warming.

Industrial Energy Production: Industrial facilities, including power plants and factories, are significant consumers of energy. The production of electricity and steam for industrial processes often involves burning fossil fuels, which leads to the emission of CO2 and other greenhouse gases.

Chemical Production: Certain chemical manufacturing processes release greenhouse gases as byproducts. For example, the production of cement generates CO2 emissions as limestone (calcium carbonate) is heated to produce lime (calcium oxide), a key component of cement.

Industrial Agriculture: Industrial agriculture contributes to global warming through the use of synthetic fertilizers, which can release nitrous oxide (N2O), a potent greenhouse gas, during their production and application. Additionally, the production of livestock, including enteric fermentation in cattle, produces methane (CH4), another potent greenhouse gas.

Waste Management: Industrial processes generate waste that can produce greenhouse gases when improperly managed. Landfills are significant sources of methane emissions as organic waste decomposes without oxygen. Similarly, wastewater treatment plants can release methane and nitrous oxide if organic matter is not properly treated.

Industrial Refrigeration and Air Conditioning: The use of hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) and other fluorinated gases in industrial refrigeration and air conditioning systems can contribute to global warming. While these gases are not as abundant as CO2, they are much more potent in terms of their global warming potential.

Deforestation and Land Use Change: Industrial activities, particularly those related to logging and land development, contribute to deforestation and land use change, as mentioned earlier. These activities release stored carbon into the atmosphere and reduce the planet's capacity to sequester CO2, further exacerbating global warming.

Overall, industrial processes are a significant source of greenhouse gas emissions, accounting for a substantial portion of anthropogenic contributions to global warming. Efforts to reduce emissions from industrial activities through improved energy efficiency, cleaner production technologies, and the transition to renewable energy sources are essential for mitigating climate change.
